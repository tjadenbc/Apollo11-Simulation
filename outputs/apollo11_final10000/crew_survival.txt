WHY THE NUMBERS DIFFER BY ASTRONAUT
----------------------------------------------------------------------
Survival diverges by which vehicle each crewman occupies during
the lunar phase. Collins (CMP) stays in the Command Module in
lunar orbit and is NOT exposed to powered-descent, surface, or
ascent risk. If the Lunar Module crew is lost, Collins's
documented contingency was to return to Earth ALONE — so an LM
loss does not kill him, though he must still complete a solo
Earth return (modeled success rate 98.8%, from this
run's own TEI+entry statistics).

Armstrong (CDR) and Aldrin (LMP) ride the Lunar Module to the
surface and back, exposed to every risk Collins faces PLUS the
descent/surface/ascent phases — so their survival is lower.

NOTE: Armstrong and Aldrin share the same vehicle for every
phase this simulation models, so their survival is identical
here. Distinguishing them would require individual-level risks
(EVA suit failure, a fall, a medical event) that are not
modeled and for which no sourced rates exist. The meaningful,
defensible distinction is Collins (CSM) vs the LM crew.

======================================================================
OUTCOME BREAKDOWN — what happened to the crew, by failure mode
======================================================================

  launch_parking_orbit_decays_into_atmosphere
     Trials with this failure: 223
     Recovery path: abort_SPS_deorbit
     Survival probability: 85%
     Crew survived (sampled): 196/223 (88%)
     Explanation:
       Perigee below 80 km — orbit will decay in hours to days, but crew
       has time. CSM separates from S-IVB and uses SPS retrograde burn to
       set up controlled entry. The 85% reflects the additional risk that
       the unstable orbit might force entry at a bad flight path angle if
       SPS can't be commanded in time.

  sm_failure_lunar_orbit
     Trials with this failure: 115
     Recovery path: lm_lifeboat_abort_lunar
     Survival probability: 85%
     Crew survived (sampled): 91/115 (79%)
     Explanation:
       Catastrophic SM failure in lunar orbit (pre-descent or post-
       ascent). LM lifeboat available for most of this window, but the
       abort starts from lunar orbit — TEI must be flown on DPS/APS with
       tighter consumables than Apollo 13's free-return geometry.
       Slightly worse than the translunar case (~85%).

  surface_lm_electrical_failure
     Trials with this failure: 96
     Recovery path: ascent_stage_cannot_arm
     Survival probability: 0%
     Crew survived (sampled): 0/96 (0%)
     Explanation:
       An unrecoverable LM electrical/switchgear failure on the surface
       prevents arming the ascent engine. Sourced anchor: Apollo 11's own
       snapped ascent-engine arming breaker (closed with a felt-tip pen)
       — 1 serious incident in 6 landings, with ~5% of such anomalies
       assumed beyond any workaround. The LM crew is stranded on the
       surface; Collins returns alone.

  launch_structural_failure_max_q_exceeded
     Trials with this failure: 140
     Recovery path: abort_mode_I_LES_late
     Survival probability: 30%
     Crew survived (sampled): 50/140 (36%)
     Explanation:
       Vehicle breakup during transonic flight (~50-70s after liftoff).
       The LES (Launch Escape System) is designed for exactly this — but
       max-Q breakup is violent and the LES requires the CM to still be
       intact when LES fires. Historical precedent: no Apollo vehicle
       ever had max-Q breakup, so this is a worst-case estimate. The 30%
       reflects: ~98% LES nominal reliability × ~30% chance the CM is
       still intact and oriented favorably after the breakup event.

  transposition_docking_failure
     Trials with this failure: 96
     Recovery path: landing_aborted_healthy_return
     Survival probability: 99%
     Crew survived (sampled): 95/96 (99%)
     Explanation:
       Transposition & docking after TLI failed unrecoverably — the LM
       cannot be extracted from the S-IVB, the landing is aborted, and
       the crew returns on a fully healthy CSM (free-return/direct abort;
       this nearly ended Apollo 14's landing before its hard-dock
       workaround succeeded). Mission failure, but survival is
       essentially that of a normal return (~99%). Same sourced ~0.95%
       per-docking rate as the rendezvous docking.

  sm_failure_transearth
     Trials with this failure: 179
     Recovery path: no_lifeboat_on_return
     Survival probability: 10%
     Crew survived (sampled): 20/179 (11%)
     Explanation:
       Catastrophic SM failure during the trans-Earth coast — the LM was
       jettisoned before TEI; there is no lifeboat. It mostly kills all
       three: the CM's entry batteries and surge tanks last hours, not
       the days usually remaining to entry. Survival (~10%) is plausible
       only when the failure strikes late enough — within the last few
       hours before entry — that the crew can ride the remaining
       consumables down and return alive.

  missed_lunar_soi_after_mcc
     Trials with this failure: 22
     Recovery path: missed_lunar_soi_after_mcc
     Survival probability: 98%
     Crew survived (sampled): 21/22 (95%)

  descent_descent_propellant_exhausted
     Trials with this failure: 116
     Recovery path: abort_stage_LM_ascent_engine
     Survival probability: 70%
     Crew survived (sampled): 76/116 (66%)
     Explanation:
       LM ran out of DPS propellant during powered descent. Apollo's
       'abort stage' procedure ignites the APS (ascent engine) while the
       descent stage is still attached, separating in-flight and putting
       the ascent stage into lunar orbit for CSM rendezvous.
       Effectiveness depends on altitude and descent rate at fuel
       exhaustion: high altitude / low descent rate → high success; close
       to surface at high vertical velocity → no time to abort. The 70%
       reflects the typical distribution of altitudes/speeds at fuel
       exhaustion in this simulation.

  surface_lm_tipover
     Trials with this failure: 44
     Recovery path: lm_tipped_no_ascent
     Survival probability: 0%
     Crew survived (sampled): 0/44 (0%)
     Explanation:
       The LM tipped beyond recovery at touchdown (0-in-6 landings
       historically against a ~12 deg stability spec; Apollo 15's 11 deg
       was the worst case). No ascent is possible from a tipped LM and no
       rescue capability exists. LM crew lost; Collins returns alone.

  entry_structural_failure_high_g
     Trials with this failure: 101
     Recovery path: CM_breakup_in_atmosphere
     Survival probability: 0%
     Crew survived (sampled): 0/101 (0%)
     Explanation:
       Atmospheric entry produced peak g-load above the Command Module's
       12 g structural limit (the simulation's
       entry_structural_failure_high_g threshold; NASA TN D-6725 cites 10
       g as the guidance limit and ~12 g structural). Structural failure
       of the heat shield or pressure vessel leads to crew loss.

  surface_eva_suit_fatality
     Trials with this failure: 8
     Recovery path: eva_fatality_partner_returns
     Survival probability: 0%
     Crew survived (sampled): 0/8 (0%)
     Explanation:
       A terminal suit/PLSS failure during the surface EVA kills one of
       the two moonwalkers (0 failures in 28 program man-EVAs; the fatal
       branch requires the anomaly to outrun the OPS backup and an
       immediate LM repress, within ~60 m of the hatch). Modeled as one
       LM-crew death (p_survive 0.5 across the exposed pair); the
       survivor and Collins return.

  rendezvous_docking_failure
     Trials with this failure: 83
     Recovery path: eva_crew_transfer
     Survival probability: 90%
     Crew survived (sampled): 77/83 (93%)
     Explanation:
       The LM closed with the CSM but the docking mechanism failed
       unrecoverably. SOURCED RATE: capture anomalies on 2 of ~21 program
       dockings (Apollo 14's six attempts; Skylab 2's interlock bypass),
       times ~10% chance the independent ring-latch hard-dock workaround
       also fails — ~0.95% unrecovered per docking. The documented
       contingency is a suited EVA crew transfer from the LM to the CSM
       side hatch, modeled at ~90% survival (estimate — planned and
       trained, never flown). Exposure is LM-crew-only; Collins is in the
       healthy CSM.

  sm_failure_translunar
     Trials with this failure: 245
     Recovery path: lm_lifeboat_abort
     Survival probability: 90%
     Crew survived (sampled): 224/245 (91%)
     Explanation:
       Catastrophic SM systems failure (Apollo-13 class: cryo tank / fuel
       cell) during the outbound coast with the LM attached. The LM-
       lifeboat abort is the demonstrated recovery — Apollo 13 survived
       exactly this — but with real margin risk (power, water, CO2
       scrubbing, entry-corridor control). ~90% survival (1-for-1
       historically, with documented thin margins). Sourced occurrence: 1
       catastrophic SM failure in 15 crewed CSM flights.

  sm_failure_surface
     Trials with this failure: 57
     Recovery path: emergency_liftoff_to_dying_csm
     Survival probability: 40%
     Crew survived (sampled): 21/57 (37%)
     Explanation:
       Catastrophic SM failure while the LM is on the surface: Collins is
       alone in the failing CSM; the LM must launch immediately (outside
       the planned liftoff window, degraded rendezvous geometry) and the
       combined stack then attempts a lifeboat return with a partially-
       expended LM. The worst pre-jettison geometry; ~40% (estimate — no
       historical analogue).

  launch_s_ivb_first_ignition_failure
     Trials with this failure: 58
     Recovery path: abort_mode_IV_COI
     Survival probability: 95%
     Crew survived (sampled): 57/58 (98%)
     Explanation:
       S-IVB failed to ignite after S-II burnout. This is exactly the
       scenario Mode IV (Contingency Orbit Insertion) was designed for:
       CSM separates, SPS burns to inject into a stable Earth parking
       orbit, then performs a normal entry and splashdown. SPS has ample
       fuel — used only for lunar maneuvers in nominal mission. Crew
       survives easily.

  descent_radar_dropout_marginal
     Trials with this failure: 4
     Recovery path: descent_radar_dropout_marginal
     Survival probability: 70%
     Crew survived (sampled): 3/4 (75%)

  launch_s_ic_underperformance_crash
     Trials with this failure: 9
     Recovery path: abort_mode_I_LES
     Survival probability: 85%
     Crew survived (sampled): 6/9 (67%)
     Explanation:
       S-IC underperformance is a SLOW failure — the rocket loses
       altitude over many seconds. EDS (Emergency Detection System) or
       crew commands LES Mode I: solid-fuel escape motor pulls CM clear
       of the stack, deploys parachutes, splashes down in Atlantic.
       Apollo Pad Abort Test 1 & 2 validated this in 1963-65. The 85%
       reflects LES nominal reliability minus edge cases (LES misfire,
       parachute failure).
